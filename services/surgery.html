Surgery & Pain Management

Pet Surgery & Pain Management in Williamsburg

Hearing that your pet needs surgery is frightening, and it's not a recommendation we make lightly. As an AAHA-accredited hospital in Williamsburg, we'll make sure you understand why — so you can decide with confidence.

What kinds of surgery do you perform?

Our surgeries fall into two groups. Elective procedures include spays, neuters, dental extractions, and removal of benign skin growths. Urgent procedures include things like wound and abscess repair, removal of swallowed objects, torn ACL/cruciate repair, fracture repair, bladder stones, and tumor removal.

Most pet surgeries are considered low risk thanks to modern veterinary medicine, and we'll always talk through the specifics of your pet's procedure with you first.

How do you keep anesthesia safe?

We follow strict AAHA anesthesia guidelines. Before surgery, we run bloodwork and, depending on your pet's health, additional tests to check for any risk of complications. Anesthesia is very safe when a patient is stabilized beforehand and the team understands the pet's condition going in.

We monitor your pet closely throughout, and afterward we'll explain what's normal during recovery, like grogginess for a few hours and deeper sleep for the first day.

How do you manage my pet's pain?

No pet should suffer when their pain can be managed. We build a pain plan around each animal, which may include medication (analgesics, anti-inflammatories), complementary therapies like laser therapy and physical therapy, and simple lifestyle changes such as softer bedding or raised food bowls.

Pain can be acute, from a recent injury, or chronic, from conditions like arthritis or dental disease. Watch for limping, hiding, loss of appetite, vocalizing, or licking one spot, and let us know early.

How should I prepare my pet, and what about recovery?

We'll give you specific pre-surgical instructions, which usually include withholding food after midnight the night before while allowing water until morning, and arriving on time. For routine procedures, most pets go home a few hours after waking up; advanced or emergency cases may stay 24 hours or longer for monitoring.

At home, give your pet a warm, quiet space, keep them from licking the incision (usually with an e-collar), watch for redness or swelling, and follow the recovery plan until we tell you otherwise.
