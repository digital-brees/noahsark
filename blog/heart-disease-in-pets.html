Heart Disease in Cats and Dogs: What to Watch For

Heart disease is more common than many owners realize. The American Veterinary Medical Association estimates it affects about 1 in 10 cats, and like many conditions, it's far more manageable when it's found early. Pets are good at hiding illness, so knowing the signs gives your companion the best chance.

What are the signs of heart problems in pets?

Watch for lethargy or weakness, tiring quickly with activity, coughing, fast breathing while resting (not panting), fainting or collapse, and an elevated heart rate. In cats, sudden paralysis or severe pain in the hind legs is an emergency, often caused by a blood clot related to heart disease, and needs to be seen right away. Many of these signs can point to other conditions too, which is exactly why they're worth a prompt exam.

What causes heart disease in cats and dogs?

Heart disease can be present at birth (congenital) or develop later in life (acquired) from age, injury, or infection. In cats, the most common form is hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM), a thickening of the heart muscle. Sometimes heart changes are secondary to another problem, such as an overactive thyroid, which can be treated.

How is it diagnosed?

We start by listening to the heart and lungs for abnormal sounds, then use tools that don't hurt your pet: an ultrasound to see the heart's chambers and valves, x-rays to check its size and the lungs, an EKG to measure electrical activity, a blood pressure reading, and bloodwork. Together these diagnostic tools give us a clear picture of what's going on and how to respond.

Can heart disease be treated?

There's no cure for HCM, but medication can do a lot, helping the heart relax, slowing the heart rate, and easing its workload to lower the risk of heart failure. When the cause is something treatable like a thyroid problem, addressing it can improve the heart symptoms too. Pets caught in the milder stages can often live comfortably for years. Consistent follow-up visits, often part of senior wellness care, and sticking to the medication plan make the biggest difference.
