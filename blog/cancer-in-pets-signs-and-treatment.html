Cancer in Dogs and Cats: What Every Owner Should Know

A cancer diagnosis is one of the hardest things to hear about a pet who's part of your family. It helps to know two things up front: cancer is the leading cause of death in dogs over the age of ten, and about half of all cancers are treatable when they're caught early. That's why knowing the signs, and keeping up with regular exams, matters so much.

What are the warning signs of cancer in pets?

Some signs are easy to spot and others hide. The most common ones include new lumps or bumps under the skin, sores that won't heal, sudden weight loss, a change in appetite, unusual odors or discharge, swelling of the belly, trouble breathing, lethargy, and changes in bathroom habits. Most owners first find a lump while petting or bathing their pet. If you feel something new, or notice any of these changes, call to have it checked rather than waiting to see if it passes.

What kinds of cancer are most common?

Dogs and cats can develop many of the same cancers people do. Some of the more common ones include lymphoma (often felt as swollen glands), mast cell tumors on the skin, osteosarcoma in the bones of larger dogs, and hemangiosarcoma. Two are largely preventable: mammary cancer, far less likely in pets spayed early, and testicular cancer, prevented by neutering. Certain breeds carry higher risk, so we sometimes recommend earlier screening.

How is cancer diagnosed?

Only a veterinarian can diagnose cancer, which is one reason routine wellness exams are so valuable. During an exam we can feel for lumps you might not see and check for the subtler signs. If we find something, the usual next step is a needle aspirate, drawing a few cells from the lump to identify what it is. That diagnosis, along with any testing we recommend, guides what comes next, whether that's monitoring, surgery, or further care.

What are the treatment options?

Treatment depends on the type of cancer, how far it's advanced, and your pet's age and overall health. Options are similar to human medicine and include surgery, chemotherapy, and radiation, sometimes alone and sometimes in combination. For a slow-growing tumor in an older pet, the kindest plan is sometimes comfort rather than aggressive treatment. We'll walk you through the trade-offs honestly so you can make the right call for your pet.
